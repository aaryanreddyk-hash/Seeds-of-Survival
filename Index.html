Overview

Crop diversity is essential for food security, nutrition, cultural identity, and agricultural resilience. As modern agriculture concentrates on fewer varieties, we lose traits that help crops survive pests, disease, and climate extremes.

About — Who, What, When, Where, Why & How

Who?

The endangerment of crop species affects farmers, consumers, indigenous communities, and the global food system as a whole. Everyone who depends on agriculture—now and in the future—is impacted by shrinking crop diversity.

What?

Many crop species and their wild relatives are becoming endangered due to rapid loss of genetic diversity. Modern agriculture's focus on a narrow set of high-yield varieties pushes locally adapted crops toward extinction, removing valuable traits like disease resistance and drought tolerance.

When?

This trend began in the mid-20th century with industrial farming and the Green Revolution. It has accelerated in recent decades through climate change, global seed markets, and large-scale monocultures.

Where?

The problem is global but especially severe in regions rich in traditional agricultural diversity — South Asia, Africa, and Latin America. Wild crop relatives are also threatened in developed regions by habitat loss.

Why?

Drivers include monoculture farming, climate change, habitat destruction, industrial seed systems, overuse of agrochemicals, and the erosion of indigenous seed-saving knowledge.

How?

Loss of crop species reduces food security, narrows dietary options, and increases vulnerability to pests, disease, and climate stress. Cultural and economic losses follow, affecting communities and global agriculture.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the purpose of this website?

To educate about threats to crop species, why diversity matters, and actions people can take.

Why are crop species becoming endangered?

Due to monoculture, climate change, habitat loss, industrial seed systems, and the decline of traditional farming practices.

Why is crop diversity important?

It provides genetics for resilience — drought tolerance, disease resistance, and adaptability to climate change.

Who is affected?

Farmers, consumers, indigenous communities, and future generations.

How can individuals help?

Support seed-saving groups, buy local and diverse produce, choose heirloom varieties, donate, and spread awareness.

Organizations working on this?

Crop Trust, FAO, CGIAR, Seed Savers Exchange, Bioversity International, and national seed banks.

What is a seed bank?

A facility that stores seeds long-term to preserve crop diversity for restoration and research.

How serious is the issue?

Very serious—substantial diversity has already been lost and urgent action is needed.

Campaigning Guide

  1. Define your goal — pick a clear, measurable objective.
  2. Identify your audience — farmers, students, consumers, etc.
  3. Craft your core message — headline, short summary, call to action.
  4. Build a timeline & roles — plan stages and responsibilities.
  5. Prepare materials — graphics, emails, flyers, forms.
  6. Choose channels & tactics — social media, email, events, partners.
  7. Launch — release your main post and ask partners to amplify it.
  8. Engage & grow momentum — share updates and host events.
  9. Partner outreach — connect with local orgs and schools.
  10. Fundraising tactics — crowdfunding, matching, incentives.
  11. Run events & collect proof — workshops, seed swaps, photos, stories.
  12. Track metrics & iterate — measure results and improve.
  13. Mid-campaign push — use urgency to re-engage supporters.
  14. Close & thank supporters — summarize results and show appreciation.
  15. Post-campaign follow-up — final reports and next steps.
